# Concurrent training — interference reference

Established training-science consensus on the "interference effect": endurance
and speed/strength adaptations compete when trained close together. Signal
quality — not just fatigue — is the cost. Framed for a three-mode athlete
(explosive / aerobic / loaded).

## aerobic-before-speed — long aerobic work blunts sprint quality

High-volume aerobic work leaves residual central and peripheral fatigue that
degrades maximal neural output for roughly 24–48 h. Sprinting is a
skill-and-signal session: it only adapts what it can express. Sprinting on
blunted legs trains slower patterns. At the molecular level the endurance
stimulus (AMPK-dominant signaling) suppresses the anabolic/neural pathway
(mTOR-mediated) for hours-to-days — the classic interference mechanism.
Practical rule: keep max-velocity sessions at least 48 h after long aerobic
sessions, or do speed FIRST on fresh legs when both must happen close together.

## recovery-windows — spacing high-intensity and high-volume sessions

Sprint sessions create adaptation demands (neural, tendon, fast-twitch) that
need their own recovery window. A long aerobic session inside ~48 h after
sprinting diverts recovery resources and dampens the adaptive signal the sprint
session was supposed to send. Order matters less than spacing: the interference
is largest when the long session lands soonest after the sprint work. Easy,
genuinely low-intensity movement is fine; long/hard is not.

## structural-fatigue — heavy loaded work before neural sessions

Loaded carries and big-vert days (rucking, mountaineering prep) produce
structural fatigue: eccentric muscle damage from descents, connective-tissue
stress from load, and gait-pattern fatigue. Sprinting within ~48 h of a big
loaded day (>800 m vert or heavy load) carries that structural fatigue into
maximal-velocity work — elevated injury risk (hamstrings especially) and poor
speed quality. Sequence big carries AFTER speed work in the microcycle, not
before.

## general — how to use these flags

None of these are "never do this" rules; they are sequencing costs. For a
sprinter rebuilding aerobic base while preparing for mountains, some
interference is the accepted price of concurrent goals. The flags exist so the
cost is chosen, not accidental.
